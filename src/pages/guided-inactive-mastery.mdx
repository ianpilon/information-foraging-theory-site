# Guided Inactive Mastery

**Summary**: A behavior-change technique where you give people a goal they know they can achieve with about 90 percent confidence, let them do it, then move them to something more difficult. The most effective intervention for building [self-efficacy](/self-efficacy) in Bandura's framework.

**Sources**: NYTECH- Models of Human-Information Interaction for UX Design 10-25-16.pdf

**Last updated**: 2026-05-20

---

## The technique

1. Give people a goal they know they can achieve with about 90 percent confidence
2. Let them do it
3. Move them to something slightly more difficult
4. Repeat

By taking the small ramp up in goals, you can get people to do things they would never have conceived of doing before.

## The Fittle study

Pirolli described a concrete deployment in the StressBuster mode of [fittle](/fittle). The system assigned exercises and meditations. Three conditions over 28 days:

| Condition | Description | Compliance after 4 weeks |
|---|---|---|
| Easy | Very simple ramp-up over four weeks | Highest among non-adaptive (people stuck around) |
| Difficult | Charged into harder exercises quickly | Lowest (dropouts) |
| Adaptive | Always trying to choose goals slightly more difficult than what worked; if you failed it gave slightly easier goals (this is guided inactive mastery) | 65 to 70 percent compliance, even though exercises were harder than the easy condition |

In other words: by personalizing the difficulty curve, you got people doing more difficult things than they would have done in the easy condition, with similar compliance.

## How it connects to the computational model

The system's policy is the practical implementation of the [self-efficacy](/self-efficacy) ACT-R model. The model predicts when a person's efficacy is high enough to take on a slightly harder task and when it has bled off enough that the system should back down.

## Why this matters

A standard kind of educational intervention is "mastery learning," where you tailor the goals of a curriculum to the individual and they only move on when they have achieved one thing. Without any radical personalization, this kind of approach already produces about a standard deviation of improvement against pre-experimental measures (stress levels, eating, physical activity, weight). With personalization driven by a computational user model, the gains compound.
