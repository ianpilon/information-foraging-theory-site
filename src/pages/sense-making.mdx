# Sense-making

**Summary**: A category of information tasks where someone is given a pile of data, has to organize and interpret it, and at the end must do something with it. Operates on the order of minutes to hours.

**Sources**: NYTECH- Models of Human-Information Interaction for UX Design 10-25-16.pdf

**Last updated**: 2026-05-20

---

## What counts as sense-making

Pirolli's working definition: tasks where someone says "here is a pile of information, I want you to figure it out, organize it, and do something with it. Make a decision, write a report, or whatever."

Three characteristics:

1. Working with large amounts of data
2. Ill-structured: you do not have a precise definition of the goal, you figure it out as you go
3. Funneled into some concrete output (a decision, a report, a recommendation)

The tasks Pirolli's group studied: understanding health problems to make a medical decision, buying a laptop, forecasting weather, helping intelligence agencies write intelligence reports.

## Two example studies

**Laptop purchase (1992):** A subject going through magazines with laptop reviews, trying to compare them, building representations on paper that make comparison easier, eventually choosing a laptop.

**Intelligence analyst:** A person with 20+ years in the intelligence agency, working with open source material, trying to figure out whether the former Soviet Union had a bio-warfare program around bioregulators. He went on PubMed, ran searches on names he had identified, produced a document of names, institutes, and topics, color-coded it, built a small social network, formed a logical argument about whether the actors were working with the military, and sketched out what would go into the report. All in about two hours. Pirolli described him as "a jet fighter of sense-making."

## The sense-making loop

Pirolli and Stuart Card developed a schematic of the process. The core idea: as the analyst expends more effort, the representation becomes increasingly structured. They build a mental model with progressively richer representations. There are two major loops running simultaneously:

1. Going out to get more information
2. Interpreting that information into some kind of mental model of what is in the data

## What gets modeled

Pirolli's group then took the broad sense-making framework and refined it into an [act-r](/act-r) model of human information processing, then refined it again into a neural computational model of these tasks. The intelligence agency that funded this work was particularly interested in what biases people are prone to in sense-making. Confirmation bias is the most prominent finding: once people form a hypothesis, they search for confirming evidence rather than disconfirming evidence. The models predict that this is what people tend to do.

A second sense-making model uses [topic-modeling-knowledge-tracing](/topic-modeling-knowledge-tracing) to trace how people come to understand a domain over time.
