# Trust in Autonomous Systems

**Summary**: The closing theme of Pirolli's 2016 talk. As AI and autonomous systems become pervasive, the UX challenge becomes helping users form accurate trust and credibility judgments about black-box systems. Models of human credibility judgment (built originally for social media) become reusable here.

**Sources**: NYTECH- Models of Human-Information Interaction for UX Design 10-25-16.pdf

**Last updated**: 2026-05-20

---

## The question that prompted this thread

A Q&A question after the talk: "how do you think these cognitive model systems are going to impact autonomous vehicles?"

## The framing

Pirolli's answer:

1. A wave of autonomous systems is coming. Driverless cars are emerging commercially. DARPA is investing heavily in autonomy as its technological bet.
2. These systems are black boxes. The Tesla accident reports illustrate that our ability to reason about them is limited. They are kinds of agents whose capabilities and limits are very hard to understand.
3. The biggest current challenge: understanding the capabilities and limits of these agents.
4. One way to get traction: model how people come to interpret the behaviors of these systems and form judgments of credibility or trust. "Can you trust this car to drive on the highway at this speed in these kinds of conditions?"

## What makes it harder

The current crop of AI is almost impossible to interpret even by the people who built it. Deep learning is the example: trying to understand what a model has learned is difficult even for its authors.

A precursor problem: filter bubbles. Fancy personalization algorithms tailor your information environment, and the people who built them often cannot tell you exactly why they work that way. That same problem will carry over to autonomous systems.

## Mixed initiative as the default

Even if the systems were perfectly automated and required no human supervision, you could ignore the trust problem. They will not be. Pirolli expects mixed-initiative situations as the norm, where:

- The system does things the user did not anticipate
- The user needs to stay engaged enough to remain vigilant
- The user needs to understand what the system is doing and where it is about to hit boundary conditions on its capabilities

This requires a computational model of the user (where their attention is, what they understand, what they are about to misjudge) and a transparent system that can communicate its own state.

## Connection back to the earlier work

The Twitter [credibility-judgments](/credibility-judgments) thread is a direct precursor. The machinery used to model how people judge social media sources can be repurposed to model how they judge autonomous systems. The [topic-modeling-knowledge-tracing](/topic-modeling-knowledge-tracing) thread also fits: a system that maintains a continuous model of what the user knows can decide when and how to surface relevant capability information.

## The forward-looking statement

Pirolli closes by saying: "we're going to have sensing, big data, quantified self that poses these amazing challenges for doing the kinds of modeling I'm talking about. Most services are going to be interested in these longer-term engagements, not like Google where they know you've come to this place and you're searching for a particular thing and we're done with you. We're talking about longer-term engagements over periods of months to years."

The user model is going to become much richer and much more complicated. The context is no longer just the desktop or the smartphone. It is the entire world.
