# Fittle

**Summary**: A smartphone app developed by Pirolli's group for personalized health behavior change. Built around small daily challenges, small teams of five to eight people, a coaching engine driven by a user model, and a conversational agent. The transcript renders the name as "fiddle."

**Sources**: NYTECH- Models of Human-Information Interaction for UX Design 10-25-16.pdf

**Last updated**: 2026-05-20

---

## Core interactive surfaces

Pirolli describes three core displays:

1. **Dashboard.** Organized around "challenges," programs where you start with simple goals and work your way up. Bar graphs show how well you are doing on the current challenge. Icons across the top indicate the goals being worked on (an activity goal, a nutrition goal). The default is to do at most two goals at a time, tailored to what someone can accomplish.

2. **Social feed.** People are organized into small teams of five to eight.

3. **Fittle Bot.** A simple AI agent that interacts with people.

## What gets measured

Very simple recording: ask people to tell us whether they did it or not. The system can also hook up to Fitbits and similar devices, but self-report is the default.

## Subject matter experts

Real subject matter experts develop content for the goals, design the programs.

## Pilot results

An early eight-week study with two participant groups:

- Sedentary people who wanted to get up and eat better
- Highly stressed people who wanted to do more exercises and meditation to reduce stress

Result: about a standard deviation of improvement against pre-experimental measures (stress levels, eating, physical activity). The majority of people ended up losing weight. Comparable to mastery learning, even without the radical personalization.

## The team effect

Particularly interesting finding: being on a good team versus a bad team accounted for about 40 percent of people's achievement. This is a phenomenon that is there and "we don't really understand and the thing that this drove us to was to try to see whether we could build more intelligent systems that could do something more like intelligent coaching."

The coaching engine sits at the core of the next-version architecture, with a model of the user, a model of the goals, an attempt to personalize, and a variety of ecological interventions (reminders, motivational messages, behavior-change techniques from the literature).
