# Behavior Change

**Summary**: Long-term lifestyle and habit-change problems, operating on the order of months to years. Pirolli's most recent research thread as of the 2016 talk, motivated by the cost and intractability of changing health-related behaviors.

**Sources**: NYTECH- Models of Human-Information Interaction for UX Design 10-25-16.pdf

**Last updated**: 2026-05-20

---

## Why this matters

Two drivers, one practical, one intellectual.

**Practical.** Roughly half a trillion dollars per year in medical costs are avoidable because they trace back to changeable behaviors: smoking, eating too much, not being active enough. Behavioral and environmental factors account for more deaths than genetics. The big precision-medicine genetics initiative addresses about 30 percent of the problem; the other 70 percent is changeable behavior. Mobile and wearable technology now makes it possible to measure and intervene in everyday life in momentary ways.

**Intellectual.** It is a deep scientific challenge. Tasks repeat daily, the change takes months or years, and you have to model not a single decision but the trajectory of a person across many decisions.

## The Hawaii case

Pirolli's group works with a large Hawaii health organization. The numbers tell the story:

- They run weight loss programs
- 81 percent of joiners lose weight
- After six months, they rebound
- The organization cannot stay engaged with people forever
- They deal with about a thousand people per year through their excellent counselors
- They have 35,000 people who currently qualify and have been recommended by their doctors
- The state geography is hostile: centers are in one place, people are remote, the organization will pay for plane tickets for weekly clinic visits because it saves money down the road

This is the kind of problem where a disruptive technology that scales could be a big win.

## The applied system

[fittle](/fittle), a smartphone app, is Pirolli's group's attempt at that disruptive intervention.

## The underlying psychology

The work draws on:

- [habit-formation](/habit-formation) neuroscience: two circuits, one in the prefrontal cortex for goal striving, one for sensorimotor habit, with a transition that takes weeks to a year of repetition
- [self-efficacy](/self-efficacy) (Bandura's social cognitive theory): you have to believe you can do something to actually do it
- [guided-inactive-mastery](/guided-inactive-mastery): a personalization technique that produced 65 to 70 percent compliance after four weeks in a small study

## Why it connects back to information foraging

The same modeling-and-simulation approach used in [information-foraging-theory](/information-foraging-theory) and [sense-making](/sense-making) gets applied here: build a detailed [act-r](/act-r) computational model of an individual, simulate their trajectory, use the simulation to personalize the intervention. The difference is timescale and content, not method.
